## Phase 1 — Opening briefs (blind; each argues against *any* other operationalization)

### Socratic Council — counsel's brief

#### Thesis

This operationalization is correct because it translates "can live without" into a quantitative viability threshold on a directly measurable channel (growth_rate), tests it across a statistically powered sample that generalizes to the genome, and discriminates model artifacts from genuine biological dispensability through known-essential controls.

#### Why this operationalization is faithful to the question

The raw question asks "Which genes can E. coli live without?" — a question with three embedded constructs:

1. **"Which genes"** → Operationalized as a stratified random sample of 30 candidate genes spanning diverse functional classes (metabolism, transport, regulation, stress-response), avoiding selection bias while remaining computationally feasible. The sample size (n=30) yields a ±7–9 percentage-point margin at 95% confidence, sufficient to distinguish a 10–20% dispensable fraction from zero.

2. **"can live without"** (dispensability/nonessentiality) → Operationalized as **growth_rate > 0.05 /h sustained over ≥3 generations** in a gene_knockout/basal vs. wildtype/basal comparison. This threshold distinguishes exponential growth (viable strain) from death/stasis (inviable). Growth_rate is the instrument's direct readout of cellular fitness; it integrates across all metabolic, biosynthetic, and regulatory processes the gene might affect. A gene whose knockout yields growth_rate ≤ 0.05 /h cannot sustain the cell — the gene is essential. A gene whose knockout yields growth_rate > 0.05 /h is dispensable under those conditions.

3. **Implicit genome-wide claim** → The question is not "is gene X dispensable?" but "which genes [plural, implying a class or fraction] are dispensable?" This requires estimating a genome-wide proportion. The sample proportion p̂ (count of nonessential genes / 30) generalizes to the genome with a confidence interval, yielding a testable claim: "10–20% of the genome is dispensable."

**Nothing essential is dropped:** The question asks about survival, which is captured by sustained positive growth. The question is implicitly about laboratory conditions (the only conditions simulable), operationalized as basal media. The question asks "which" in a classificatory sense (essential vs. nonessential), operationalized as a binary threshold on a continuous observable.

**Nothing is smuggled in:** The 10–20% range comes from the literature (cited in the claim), but the operationalization does not assume this answer — it tests it. The hypothesis H1 predicts 10–20%; H0 predicts 0%. The decision rule rejects H1 if fewer than 3/30 genes are nonessential (i.e., <10%). The known-essential controls (ftsZ, dnaA, rpoB, rpoD, murC) are explicitly separated and serve only to validate model fidelity, not to inflate the dispensable count.

#### Why it is decisive

**Falsifiability:** H1 makes a precise numerical prediction (3–6 of 30 candidate genes nonessential) and names a concrete refutation condition: if 0, 1, or 2 candidate genes are nonessential, H1 is rejected. This is a sharp, binary outcome. The null hypothesis H0 (all genes essential) predicts zero viable knockouts; a single nonessential gene refutes H0. The hypotheses are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive over the observable space (count of viable knockouts).

**Discriminating power against rivals:**

- **Rival 1 (model-artifact hypothesis):** If apparent dispensability is an artifact of model incompleteness, then known-essential genes (ftsZ, dnaA, rpoB, rpoD, murC) should show growth_rate > 0.05 /h in their knockouts (the model fails to capture their essentiality). The operationalization includes these 5 controls explicitly and rejects H1 if *any* of them remains viable. Conversely, if all 5 known-essential genes fail and known-nonessential genes (lacZ, malT under basal conditions with no lactose/maltose) survive, the model correctly captures essentiality and the rival is refuted. This is a direct mechanistic check on model validity.

- **Rival 2 (condition-dependence hypothesis):** If essentiality is entirely condition-dependent (no genes are universally dispensable), then the set of nonessential genes should shrink dramatically under stress. The operationalization includes a 10-gene subset re-screened under condition=acetate. If the nonessential fraction collapses (e.g., 4/10 → 0/10), the rival is supported; if it remains stable (4/10 in both basal and acetate), a core dispensable fraction exists and the rival is refuted. This is a direct comparison of dispensability profiles across conditions.

**Feasibility:** The design is computationally tractable: 35 gene knockouts × 8 seeds × 5 generations = 1,400 simulations, well within instrument capacity. The growth_rate channel is a core model output, always available. The perturbation gene_knockout is explicitly listed as available. The basal condition is the reference design. The decision rule requires only counting and a simple proportion calculation, no complex statistics.

#### Why any other operationalization is weaker

**Alternative 1: "A gene is dispensable if its knockout does not crash the simulation."**  
*Failure mode:* Simulation failure (non-convergence) may reflect numerical instability rather than biological lethality. Some gene knockouts may yield very low but positive growth_rate (e.g., 0.01 /h) without crashing — the cell is technically "alive" but not viable over evolutionary timescales. This operationalization conflates numerical success with biological viability. The growth_rate > 0.05 /h threshold is more faithful because it distinguishes sustained exponential growth (viable) from near-stasis (effectively dead). Conversely, this operationalization would correctly classify a gene as essential if the knockout crashes, but it would *miss* essential genes whose knockout produces a numerically stable but biologically inviable slow-growth state.

**Alternative 2: "A gene is dispensable if its knockout yields growth_rate within 10% of wildtype."**  
*Failure mode:* This is too stringent. A gene may be nonessential (the cell survives without it) but its knockout may reduce growth_rate by 30–50% due to metabolic inefficiency or regulatory burden. The biological question is "can live without," not "can thrive without." A 50% growth-rate reduction still represents a viable strain; the gene is dispensable. This alternative would incorrectly classify many truly dispensable genes as essential. It also introduces an arbitrary threshold (10%) that lacks biological justification. The 0.05 /h threshold, by contrast, is grounded in the distinction between exponential growth (positive fitness) and stasis/death (zero fitness).

**Alternative 3: "Screen all ~4,000 E. coli genes individually."**  
*Failure mode:* Computationally infeasible (4,000 knockouts × 8 seeds × 5 generations = 160,000 simulations). More critically, it is statistically unnecessary: a stratified random sample of 30 genes yields a ±7–9 percentage-point margin at 95% confidence, sufficient to distinguish a 10–20% dispensable fraction from 0% or from 30–40%. The loss of precision from sampling is negligible compared to the gain in feasibility. The alternative also provides no additional falsifiability — it answers the same hypothesis but at prohibitive cost.

**Alternative 4: "A gene is dispensable if its knockout shows dry_mass > 0 at the final time point."**  
*Failure mode:* Dry mass accumulation does not necessarily indicate sustained growth. A dying culture may maintain or even increase dry_mass for one generation as ribosomes/proteins degrade slowly, but growth_rate approaches zero. This observable is less faithful to "can live" (which implies sustained proliferation) than growth_rate, which directly measures the rate of exponential increase. Additionally, dry_mass is a state variable (amount at one time) rather than a rate (change over time); it does not distinguish a stagnant culture (nonviable) from a growing one (viable).

**Alternative 5: "Count genes whose knockout reduces protein_mass or rna_mass below a threshold."**  
*Failure mode:* These channels measure biomass components, not fitness. A gene knockout may reduce protein_mass by 20% but still yield a viable strain with positive growth_rate (the cell compensates by reallocating resources). Conversely, a gene knockout may maintain normal protein_mass initially but crash after 2 generations as a critical pathway collapses. These observables are downstream consequences of viability, not direct measures of it. Growth_rate integrates across all cellular processes (anabolism, catabolism, replication) and is the canonical metric of fitness in microbiology.

**Alternative 6: "Define essentiality as growth_rate = 0 exactly, not ≤ 0.05 /h."**  
*Failure mode:* In a stochastic simulator, growth_rate may fluctuate near zero due to noise. A threshold of exactly zero is numerically fragile: a gene knockout might yield growth_rate = 0.001 /h in one replicate and −0.002 /h in another (numerical jitter). The 0.05 /h threshold provides a noise buffer, ensuring the classification is robust to replicate variability. It also has biological meaning: 0.05 /h is a doubling time of ~14 hours, far slower than wildtype (~1 /h, doubling time ~40 min). A strain growing at 0.04 /h cannot compete in any realistic environment and is functionally inviable.

**Alternative 7: "Test essentiality only under stress conditions (acetate, amino-acid depletion)."**  
*Failure mode:* This answers a different question — "which genes are essential under stress?" — not "which genes can E. coli live without?" The raw question is implicitly about survival in general, which is most neutrally tested under permissive (basal) conditions. Testing only under stress would underestimate the dispensable fraction (more genes become essential under stress) and would not establish a baseline genome-wide proportion. The operationalization correctly addresses condition-dependence as a rival hypothesis (Rival 2) by testing a subset under acetate, but the primary claim is about basal dispensability.

#### Concessions

1. **The 0.05 /h threshold is pragmatic, not canonical.** There is no universal biological definition of "minimum viable growth rate." The choice of 0.05 /h (doubling time ~14 h) is defended as a buffer above zero that accommodates noise while excluding functionally dead strains, but a threshold of 0.03 /h or 0.10 /h would be equally defensible. Sensitivity analysis (re-classifying genes at multiple thresholds) would strengthen confidence, but is not included in the current design.

### Cellarium agent — counsel's brief

#### Thesis
This operationalization is correct because it defines gene essentiality as the empirically measurable consequence of knockout on organism-level fitness (growth rate), tested genome-wide under standardized conditions with an explicit quantitative threshold that separates biologically meaningful impairment from noise.

#### Why this operationalization is faithful to the question

The raw question "Which genes can E. coli live without?" contains three constructs requiring operationalization:

1. **"Which genes"** → mapped to a representative 50+ gene sample spanning metabolic (lacZ, galE, araA, xylA), regulatory (crp, fnr, arcA, rpoS, lexA), and structural (ompF, fliC, fimA, acrB) functional categories, tested individually via `gene_knockout` perturbation with named `target_genes` parameter.

2. **"live without"** → mapped to `growth_rate > 0` (viability) AND `growth_rate ≥ 0.8 × wildtype` (non-essential). This dual criterion captures both survival (positive growth) and functional sufficiency (near-wildtype performance). The 0.8 threshold operationalizes "live without" as tolerating ≤20% fitness cost, distinguishing dispensable genes from those whose loss causes substantial but sub-lethal impairment.

3. **"can… live"** → evaluated at generation 10 under `basal` minimal media, representing physiological steady-state without confounding environmental complexity. The `basal` condition provides the canonical laboratory reference state where "living" means sustained exponential growth in defined nutrients.

The claim's "substantial fraction ≥30%" operationalizes the implicit scope question ("which genes" = how many? what proportion?) into a testable genome-wide statistic, preventing cherry-picking of individual cases.

Nothing essential is dropped: the question asks about gene necessity, captured by knockout effect on viability/fitness. Nothing is smuggled in: the 30% threshold and 20% growth cutoff are declared up-front as the quantitative meaning of "substantial" and "live without," not discovered post-hoc.

#### Why it is decisive

**Falsifiability**: H1 predicts `fraction_non_essential ≥ 0.30`; H0 predicts `< 0.30`. The decision rule computes the exact fraction from knockout screen data and applies binomial 95% CI, rejecting H1 if the confidence interval excludes 0.30. This is a sharp, one-tailed test with no wiggle room.

**Discriminating power against rivals**:
- **Rival 1 (condition-dependence)**: Distinguished by running identical gene set in `acetate` vs. `basal` conditions and computing fraction_non_essential separately, with McNemar test on paired gene classifications. If essentiality is truly condition-invariant, the fractions and gene identities should be stable; if condition-dependent, acetate will show <20% non-essential and significant gene reclassification. The `disconfirm(gene_knockout/acetate, gene_knockout/basal, growth_rate)` call on matched genes directly tests whether the same gene's "dispensability" status flips.

- **Rival 2 (temporal instability)**: Distinguished by extending simulation to 30 generations for a subset (30 genes) and tracking `growth_rate` trajectory. If "non-essential" at gen 10 is an artifact of delayed lethality, ≥50% of gen-10 non-essential genes will show >20% decline by gen 30 or fall below 0.5× wildtype. The `disconfirm(gene_knockout/basal/gen30, wildtype/basal/gen30, growth_rate)` on the subset isolates late-onset fitness collapse.

**Feasibility**: The instrument directly supports `gene_knockout` perturbations with explicit `target_genes` lists, `wildtype` reference, `basal` and `acetate` conditions, and `growth_rate` readout at specified `generations`. The 50-gene screen × 5 seeds × 10 generations is computationally tractable; the designs are exact instantiations of the schema (perturbation=`gene_knockout`, params=`{"target_genes": [list]}`). The `disconfirm()` API returns `welch_t ≥ 2` for each KO vs. wildtype, providing per-gene statistical power, and `effect_pct` quantifies the relative growth impairment directly.

#### Why any other operationalization is weaker

**Alternative 1: "Viability = growth_rate > 0 only" (no fitness threshold)**
- *Failure mode*: A gene whose knockout reduces growth_rate to 0.05× wildtype (95% impairment) would be classified "non-essential" despite the cell being functionally crippled. The question "can live without" implies not just bare survival but functional self-sufficiency; a bacterium growing 20× slower than wildtype is ecologically irrelevant and arguably not "living without" the gene in any meaningful sense. This operationalization inflates non-essentiality by including near-lethal knockouts.

**Alternative 2: "Measure final cell_mass or protein_mass instead of growth_rate"**
- *Failure mode*: Mass accumulation conflates growth rate with duration. A slow-growing knockout may reach high mass by gen 10 simply by dividing less frequently (longer cell cycle); `cell_mass` at a fixed generation does not distinguish a viable-but-slow mutant from a dying cell that temporarily accumulates mass before collapse. `growth_rate` (instantaneous 1/h) is the direct readout of fitness and doubling time, the canonical metric of bacterial "living."

**Alternative 3: "Test fewer genes (e.g., 10-20 representative genes)"**
- *Failure mode*: With n=10 genes, the binomial 95% CI on fraction_non_essential is ±30 percentage points (e.g., 3/10 = 30% with CI [7%, 65%]), making the test inconclusive for distinguishing 30% from 50% or 30% from 15%. The claim's "≥30%" requires sufficient sample size to resolve the boundary; n≥50 narrows the CI to ~±14 points (95% CI for 15/50 = [17%, 43%]), enabling decisive rejection if true fraction is <20% or confident support if >40%.

**Alternative 4: "Require growth_rate ≥ 0.95× wildtype (stricter threshold)"**
- *Failure mode*: The 5% threshold approaches measurement noise (stochastic variation across seeds) and mechanistic sloppiness in the simulator's gene-knockout implementation. The instrument's `disconfirm()` uses `welch_t ≥ 2` as the noise floor; requiring differences <5% risks classifying genes as "essential" due to intrinsic replicate variability rather than true biological deficit. The 20% threshold (0.8×) balances biological relevance (substantial fitness cost) with statistical resolvability, validated by the auxiliary assumption that this exceeds noise.

**Alternative 5: "Test essentiality under multiple carbon sources simultaneously (acetate + basal + fumarate)"**
- *Failure mode*: Broader != better. The raw question asks "can E. coli live without" (singular organism, singular condition); introducing multiple conditions without specifying which is canonical makes the claim ambiguous (essential in ANY condition? ALL conditions? MOST conditions?). The operationalization anchors to `basal` as the reference physiological state, with `acetate` tested separately as Rival 1 to explicitly address condition-dependence rather than smuggling it into the null. Testing 3+ conditions dilutes statistical power (gene × condition combinatorics) without clarifying the construct.

**Alternative 6: "Define non-essential as 'knockout exhibits positive growth in at least one condition' (existential quantifier)"**
- *Failure mode*: This inverts the burden: a gene is "non-essential" if we can find ANY environment where the cell survives its absence, even if it's lethal in 99% of conditions. This operationalization answers "are there conditions where the gene is dispensable?" rather than "is the gene dispensable under standard laboratory growth?" — a categorically different (and weaker) question. The raw question's implicit scope is the organism's normal lifestyle, operationalized here as `basal` minimal media.

#### Concessions

1. **Representativeness assumption**: The 50-gene sample may not perfectly mirror genome-wide essentiality distribution. Genes were selected to span functional categories, but sampling bias (e.g., overrepresentation of well-characterized genes, undersampling of hypothetical ORFs) could skew the fraction_non_essential estimate. If the simulator lacks mechanistic representation for 30% of the genome (e.g., prophage genes, poorly annotated ORFs), the tested fraction applies only to the modeled subset, not the full 4,300-gene genome.

2. **Threshold arbitrariness**: The 0.8× wildtype cutoff (20% impairment) is a pragmatic choice, not a natural kind. A gene causing 19% growth reduction is "non-essential" while 21% is "essential" — the biology likely lies on a continuum. Alternative defensible thresholds (0.9×, 0.7×) would shift the fraction_non_essential and potentially flip the hypothesis outcome. The 20% value is justified by signal-to-noise considerations and precedent in fitness-cost literature, but remains a researcher degree of freedom.

3. **Generation-10 snapshot vulnerability**: Even with Rival 2 testing gen-30 stability for a subset, the primary claim rests on gen-10 data. Genes classified non-essential at gen 10 could show delayed epistatic collapse (synthetic lethality with accumulated suppressor mutations) or environmental sensitivity not captured by basal→acetate contrast. The 10-generation horizon is an auxiliary assumption about equilibration time, not validated within the operationalization itself.

4. **Binary classification lossy**: Collapsing continuous `growth_rate` ratios into binary essential/non-essential discards information. A gene with 0.81× wildtype growth is lumped with 1.0×, while 0.79× is lumped with 0.1×. A more nuanced operationalization (growth-rate distribution, multi-tier classification) would preserve granularity but sacrifice the decisiveness of a single percentage threshold for hypothesis testing.

5. **Simulator fidelity unverified**: The operationalization assumes `gene_knockout` perturbations faithfully simulate biological nulls (complete loss-of-function, no polar effects, no suppressor evolution within 10 generations). If the simulator's gene network is incomplete or inaccurate for some genes (e.g., missing regulatory feedback, mis-parameterized kinetics), the measured `growth_rate` may not reflect biological reality. This is a general limitation of in-silico experiments, partially mitigated by testing well-characterized genes with known phenotypes, but not eliminable within the operationalization itself.
