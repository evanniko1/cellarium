## Phase 1 — Opening briefs (blind; each argues against *any* other operationalization)

### Socratic Council — counsel's brief

#### Thesis

This operationalization is correct because it directly quantifies population-level asynchrony in the diauxic switch through time-resolved, per-seed growth resumption kinetics and applies rigorous statistical tests (CV and bimodality) that discriminate stochastic heterogeneity from synchronous, deterministic transitions.

---

#### Why this operationalization is faithful to the question

The raw question asks whether "all cells switch to the second sugar **at the same time**." This maps cleanly onto three testable constructs:

1. **"All cells" vs. heterogeneous subpopulations**: Operationalized as the **fraction of seeds** (isogenic replicates sampling intrinsic stochasticity) that resume growth within a defined window. The measure "≥30% of seeds remain growth-arrested at 120 min" directly tests whether a substantial subpopulation fails to switch synchronously.

2. **"Switch"**: Operationalized as **resumption of exponential growth** (growth_rate >0.3/h) after glucose depletion, because growth_rate integrates the cell's metabolic commitment to lactose catabolism (lac-operon induction, lactose uptake/cleavage, glycolytic flux). The transition from <0.2/h (arrested) to >0.3/h (exponential) is the phenotypic signature of the diauxic switch.

3. **"At the same time"**: Operationalized as **synchrony**, measured by:
   - **CV of resumption times** across seeds (CV <0.2 = synchronous, all within ~20 min; CV ≥0.3 = asynchronous, ≥30 min spread).
   - **Bimodality** in the snapshot distribution of growth_rate at 90 min post-depletion (Hartigan dip test p<0.05), which reveals whether the population is split into "switched" vs. "not-yet-switched" subpopulations at a fixed timepoint.

No essential element is dropped: the question's temporal claim ("at the same time") is captured by the CV statistic, the population claim ("all cells") by the fraction metric and bimodality test, and the biological event ("switch to second sugar") by growth_rate resumption. Nothing is smuggled in: the hypothesis does not presuppose a *mechanistic* source of heterogeneity (lac-operon noise vs. ppGpp vs. cell-cycle), only that asynchrony exists and is quantifiable.

---

#### Why it is decisive

**Falsifiability**: The operationalization specifies two **independent, quantitative criteria** that must both be met to support H1:
- CV ≥0.3 (30% spread in resumption times)
- Dip test p<0.05 (bimodal distribution at 90 min)

If **either** fails (CV <0.2 or p≥0.05), H0 (synchronous switching) is supported. The dual-criterion structure guards against false positives: a unimodal distribution with high CV (e.g., gradual, uniform drift) or a bimodal artifact from low sample noise both fail to confirm asynchrony.

**Discriminating power against rivals**:
- **Rival 1 (synchronous, deterministic cAMP-CRP signaling)**: The crp knockout should abolish switching entirely (growth_rate <0.2/h at all times), but in wildtype, this rival predicts CV <0.2 and dip p≥0.05. The operationalization directly tests this: if wildtype shows CV ≥0.3 and p<0.05, the rival is refuted because a uniform cAMP threshold cannot explain bimodal, asynchronous resumption.
  
- **Rival 2 (ppGpp heterogeneity as the dominant source)**: The ppGpp clamp (2× basal) should eliminate asynchrony if ppGpp noise drives the heterogeneity (CV <0.2, p≥0.05 in clamped condition). The relA/spoT double knockout should show reduced asynchrony if ppGpp dynamics are causal, but retain asynchrony if other sources dominate. The operationalization runs both controls and measures the same metrics (CV, dip test), enabling clean mechanistic discrimination.

**Feasibility**: 
- **growth_rate** is a native, high-fidelity readout sampled every 60 s, providing fine-grained temporal resolution.
- The timeline (glucose ramp-down at 1200 s over 600 s, lactose present throughout) mimics classic diauxie and is expressible in the instrument's timeline syntax.
- 50 seeds provide sufficient statistical power for the dip test (which requires n≥50 for reliable unimodality rejection) and CV estimation.
- The 200-min (12000 s) simulation window captures the predicted ~100 min mean + ~50 min tail of resumption times, ensuring that late resumers are not censored.

---

#### Why any other operationalization is weaker

**Alternative 1: Measure lac-operon mRNA or LacZ protein concentration instead of growth_rate**

*Why weaker*: 
- **Less decisive**: Operon induction (lacZ mRNA spike) does not guarantee functional switching. Cells may induce lacZ but fail to achieve sustained growth if lactose uptake is limiting, ribosomes are unavailable, or metabolic bottlenecks persist. Growth_rate integrates the *entire* metabolic cascade (uptake, catabolism, biosynthesis) and thus is the ground-truth phenotype of the switch.
- **Failure mode**: A population could show bimodal lacZ expression but unimodal growth resumption if post-translational bottlenecks (e.g., LacY permease assembly, glycolytic enzyme allocation) synchronize the final commitment to lactose metabolism. The operationalization would falsely confirm asynchrony at the transcriptional level but miss phenotypic synchrony, misrepresenting the raw question ("do cells switch").

**Alternative 2: Define "switching" as the timepoint when growth_rate first exceeds 0.1/h (lower threshold)**

*Why weaker*:
- **Less faithful**: A threshold of 0.1/h is near the noise floor of growth_rate fluctuations during lag phase and may capture transient metabolic "twitches" rather than committed exponential growth. The 0.3/h threshold (approximately half of typical E. coli exponential rate on lactose, ~0.6/h) ensures that the cell has achieved sustained, robust catabolism.
- **Failure mode**: Lowering the threshold inflates the resumption-time spread (higher CV) by including spurious early "resumptions" that revert to lag, yielding a false-positive for asynchrony. Conversely, a too-high threshold (e.g., 0.5/h) might miss cells that resume slowly but stably, underestimating heterogeneity.

**Alternative 3: Use only the CV statistic (drop the bimodality test)**

*Why weaker*:
- **Less decisive**: CV alone cannot distinguish *bimodal* asynchrony (a subpopulation of non-switchers vs. switchers) from *unimodal* asynchrony (a gradual, broad distribution with no distinct phenotypic classes). The raw question's implication ("all cells" vs. not) suggests a categorical distinction that bimodality captures but CV does not.
- **Failure mode**: A unimodal distribution with CV = 0.3 (e.g., a normal distribution with 30% spread) might arise from extrinsic noise in glucose depletion timing or minor pre-existing metabolic heterogeneity, rather than the qualitatively distinct "switcher/non-switcher" states implied by stochastic lac-operon bistability. The dip test strengthens the claim by requiring phenotypic bimodality, not just temporal spread.

**Alternative 4: Measure synchrony at a single snapshot time (e.g., 90 min) without computing per-seed resumption times**

*Why weaker*:
- **Less faithful to the temporal claim**: The raw question asks about timing ("at the same time"), which the snapshot-only approach discards. A snapshot can reveal bimodality (switched vs. not-switched subpopulations) but cannot quantify the *spread* in resumption times, which is the direct operationalization of "at the same time."
- **Failure mode**: If all cells eventually switch but with a broad temporal spread (e.g., 30–120 min), a late snapshot (e.g., 150 min) might show unimodal growth_rate (all cells now growing), missing the asynchrony. The per-seed resumption-time CV captures this directly.

**Alternative 5: Use a narrower definition of "asynchrony" (e.g., CV ≥0.5 or fraction non-switchers ≥50%)**

*Why weaker*:
- **Less sensitive**: Setting a higher bar (CV ≥0.5 or fraction ≥50%) risks false negatives. Empirical diauxie studies show heterogeneity with ~20–40% non-switchers and CV ~0.3–0.4. A stricter threshold would declare this "synchronous," contradicting the biological phenomenon the question targets.
- **Less falsifiable**: If H1 predicts CV ~0.3–0.5 but the test requires CV ≥0.5, the operationalization can fail to detect real asynchrony, reducing discriminating power.

**Alternative 6: Measure ppGpp concentration or cAMP-CRP activity instead of growth_rate**

*Why weaker*:
- **Less faithful**: The raw question is about the *behavioral* switch (cells resuming growth on lactose), not about upstream signaling states. ppGpp and cAMP-CRP are *mechanisms* that may drive asynchrony, but heterogeneity in these signals does not guarantee heterogeneous *growth* resumption if downstream steps (operon induction, enzyme expression) are deterministic or fast.
- **Failure mode**: Bimodal ppGpp at glucose depletion might reflect pre-existing stringent-response heterogeneity that is later "averaged out" by deterministic operon induction, yielding synchronous growth resumption. The operationalization would falsely attribute asynchrony to the switch itself rather than to transient metabolic heterogeneity.

#### Thesis

This operationalization is correct because it directly measures the population-level consequence of asynchronous switching — bimodal growth-rate distributions at intermediate timepoints — using a statistic (Hartigan's dip test) that discriminates unimodal smooth transitions from discrete subpopulation coexistence, while the cross-seed design isolates intrinsic cell-to-cell variability from mere measurement noise.

#### Why this operationalization is faithful to the question

The raw question asks whether "all cells switch to the second sugar at the same time." This maps onto four constructs:

1. **"Preferred sugar runs out"** → `timeline='0 minimal+acetate, 600 minimal+acetate'`: glucose (implicit in minimal media) is exhausted at generation ~600s, leaving acetate as the sole carbon source. The diauxic shift — the experimental paradigm where E. coli sequentially consumes two sugars — is the canonical context for this question.

2. **"Switch to the second sugar"** → `growth_rate` (1/h): switching means resuming exponential growth on acetate. Growth rate is the direct, immediate readout of a cell's metabolic state transition from lag (near-zero growth while rewiring metabolism) to resumed growth (acetate catabolism active). This is the gold-standard observable for phenotypic switching in microbiology.

3. **"All cells… at the same time"** (synchrony vs. asynchrony) → bimodality in the cross-seed distribution of `growth_rate` at a fixed lag-phase timepoint, quantified by Hartigan's dip test (p < 0.05) and coefficient of variation (CV ≥ 0.6). If switching is synchronous, the population's growth-rate distribution shifts smoothly and remains unimodal. If asynchronous, the population splits into lag and resumed subpopulations, producing bimodality *during* the transition. The dip test is a principled non-parametric detector of multimodality; the CV threshold ensures the spread is not just noise but reflects discrete subpopulations.

4. **"Do all cells"** (population-level claim) → ≥50 seeds (stochastic replicates): each seed is an independent realization of the same genotype in the same conditions, isolating intrinsic noise (stochastic gene expression, metabolic fluctuations) from extrinsic noise (environmental variation). The question asks about cell-to-cell variation, which is *only* observable across replicates in a whole-cell simulator where each seed is a single-cell trajectory.

Nothing essential is dropped: the question is about timing (same time vs. different times), which manifests as population structure (unimodal vs. bimodal). Nothing is smuggled in: the hypothesis does not assume a mechanism (ppGpp, stochastic transcription, pre-existing heterogeneity are all compatible with asynchrony) — it operationalizes only the phenotypic signature of asynchrony itself.

#### Why it is decisive

**Falsifiability**: H0 (synchronous switching) predicts unimodal distributions at all timepoints (dip p ≥ 0.05, CV < 0.6). H1 (asynchronous switching) predicts transient bimodality during the lag phase (dip p < 0.05, CV ≥ 0.6) with distinct lag (<0.2/h) and resumed (≥0.5/h) subpopulations. The decision rule includes a minority-class threshold (≥20%) to exclude trivial bimodality from outliers. The predictions are quantitatively distinct and mutually exclusive.

**Discriminating power against rivals**:

- **Rival 1 (synchronous deterministic)**: Predicts unimodal distributions (dip p ≥ 0.05, CV < 0.3) at all timepoints. The dip test and CV cutoffs cleanly separate this from H1.

- **Rival 2 (stochastic gene expression alone, independent of ppGpp)**: Predicts bimodality persists even when ppGpp is clamped at basal. Design `ppgpp_conc/basal_diauxic_clamp` tests this directly: if bimodality disappears under the clamp (dip p ≥ 0.05 or CV < 0.4), Rival 2 is refuted and H1 is supported (ppGpp dynamics coordinate switching). If bimodality persists, Rival 2 is supported. This is a clean mechanistic discriminator.

- **Rival 3 (pre-existing heterogeneity from exponential phase)**: Predicts bimodality arises from variation in pre-shift ribosome or metabolite pools, not from the switching process. Stratifying seeds by `ribosome_conc` at glucose depletion and re-testing within each tercile: if H1 is true, bimodality persists within each tercile (switching generates new heterogeneity during lag); if Rival 3 is true, each tercile becomes unimodal (heterogeneity was pre-existing and merely propagated). This is a conditional-independence test that isolates dynamic from static sources of variation.

**Feasibility**: The instrument provides `growth_rate`, `ppgpp_conc`, `ribosome_conc` as direct readouts. The timeline perturbation supports mid-run media shifts. The design requires 60 seeds × 4 conditions (wildtype/basal diauxic, wildtype/basal exponential, ppGpp clamp, stratified analysis) = ~240 simulations at 10 generations each — computationally modest. Hartigan's dip test is implemented in standard libraries (R: `diptest`, Python: `PyDIP`) and has known power properties: n=60 is sufficient to detect bimodality with ≥20% in the minority mode and modes separated by ≥0.3/h (well within the 0.2 vs 0.5/h thresholds). #### Why any other operationalization is weaker

**Alternative 1 (broader): "Measure population-average growth rate over time; asynchrony means slower recovery."**

- **Failure mode**: Population averages destroy the signature of subpopulations. A bimodal distribution (50% at 0/h, 50% at 1/h) has the same mean (0.5/h) as a unimodal distribution centered at 0.5/h. This operationalization cannot distinguish asynchronous switching from synchronous slow switching. It is *less faithful* (the question asks whether cells switch "at the same time," which requires resolving individual-cell or single-seed states, not just the population mean) and *not falsifiable* (cannot discriminate H0 from H1).

**Alternative 2 (narrower): "Measure the variance or standard deviation of growth rate; asynchrony means higher variance."**

- **Failure mode**: Variance quantifies spread but not *shape*. A unimodal distribution with high variance (e.g., noisy but synchronous switching with broad lag times) can have the same variance as a bimodal distribution. The dip test explicitly detects multimodality, not just spread, making it *more decisive*. Additionally, variance is sensitive to outliers; the dip test is robust. This alternative is *less falsifiable* because it does not cleanly separate bimodal from unimodal-but-variable scenarios.

**Alternative 3 (different observable): "Measure expression of acetate-utilization genes (e.g., acs mRNA) instead of growth rate."**

- **Failure mode**: Gene expression is *upstream* of phenotype. A cell with induced `acs` mRNA may not yet be growing (translation lag, metabolic rewiring lag). Growth rate is the integrated readout of whether the switch is *functionally complete*. Measuring `acs` mRNA requires species-level queries (`mrna["acs"]`), which are feasible but further from the question's phenotypic intent ("switch to the second sugar" means *using* it, not just expressing the genes). This is *less faithful* (the question is about functional switching, not molecular induction) and *potentially confounded* (mRNA may be bimodal even if growth is synchronous, or vice versa, due to post-transcriptional delays).

**Alternative 4 (different statistic): "Use a mixture-model fit (e.g., Gaussian mixture) to detect bimodality."**

- **Failure mode**: Mixture models require choosing the number of components *a priori* (is it 2-component? 3-component?) and can overfit noise, especially with n=60. Hartigan's dip test is non-parametric and tests the *null of unimodality* without assuming the shape of the alternative, making it *more falsifiable* (it avoids the researcher degrees of freedom in model selection). Mixture models are also computationally expensive and may not converge with small n. The dip test is *more feasible* and *more conservative* (it has known Type I error control; mixture models can spuriously "find" modes in noisy unimodal data).

**Alternative 5 (different design): "Run fewer seeds (e.g., n=20) to save computation."**

- **Failure mode**: The dip test's power drops sharply below n=50. With n=20, even a true bimodal distribution (40% vs 40% in two modes) may fail to reject unimodality (p ≥ 0.05) due to low power. This is *less decisive* (higher false-negative rate). The 60-seed design is justified by power analysis (auxiliary assumption #6) and is still computationally feasible (~10 min per seed on typical hardware).

**Alternative 6 (different timeline): "Use a discrete carbon-source switch (e.g., '0 minimal+glucose, 600 minimal+acetate') instead of continuous acetate."**

- **Failure mode**: The instrument's `timeline` perturbation does not support discrete carbon-source switches — the note explicitly says "NOT a carbon-source switch." The timeline '0 minimal+acetate, 600 minimal+acetate' is the closest feasible operationalization: acetate is present throughout, and glucose (implicit in minimal) depletes by generation 600s.
