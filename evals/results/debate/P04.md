## Phase 1 — Opening briefs (blind; each argues against *any* other operationalization)

### Socratic Council — counsel's brief

#### Thesis
This operationalization is correct because it maps the vague notion of "helping the population" to population biomass recovery after environmental perturbation, discriminates adaptive bet-hedging from cell-cycle artifacts and damage through phenotypic distribution shape and lineage-specific recovery dynamics, and tests the counterfactual (homogeneous population) directly via ppGpp clamping.

#### Why this operationalization is faithful to the question

**Mapping of constructs:**

- **"Some cells grow slowly"** → lowest tertile of `growth_rate` at generation 3 (pre-shift); the instrument directly measures instantaneous specific growth rate, making slow-growers an observable subpopulation rather than an inferred category.

- **"Stay prepared for a change"** → preparedness operationalized as *differential recovery capacity*: slow-tertile lineages tracked from generation 3 to generation 8 measure whether slow growth at t₀ predicts faster `dry_mass` recovery post-shift (generation 4→8 fold-change).

- **"Help a population"** → population-mean `dry_mass` at generation 8 (post-shift recovery plateau); this converts "help" from a teleological handwave into a quantitative biomass advantage, directly addressing whether heterogeneity enhances collective fitness.

- **"Can it ever"** → the environmental shift operationalization (basal→basal_minus_leucine at t=1800s) creates the "ever" condition; a constant environment cannot test adaptive value of preparedness, so the timeline perturbation is essential.

- **Phenotypic heterogeneity (the mechanism enabling some-vs.-others)** → bimodality detected via Hartigan's dip test and coefficient of variation of `growth_rate` across isogenic seeds; this tests whether the population exhibits discrete strategic phenotypes (bet-hedging) versus homogeneous noise.

**Nothing essential is dropped:** The question asks *whether* slow/prepared cells can help, not *how often* or *under which evolutionary conditions* such strategies would evolve. Testing a single downshift scenario is sufficient for existence proof. The question does not specify mechanism (ppGpp is one candidate), so the operationalization appropriately remains agnostic about upstream regulation while testing the phenotypic consequence.

**Nothing is smuggled in:** The claim does not assert slow-growers are *intentionally* prepared (evolutionary language avoided); it tests only whether pre-shift growth-rate variance correlates with post-shift recovery advantage. The ppGpp clamp is justified as a heterogeneity-suppression tool, not a claim about molecular mechanism (see auxiliary assumptions acknowledging pleiotropic risk).

#### Why it is decisive

**Falsifiability:**
- **Step 1** (bimodality test): H1 predicts dip-test p < 0.05 in wildtype; p ≥ 0.05 falsifies immediately.
- **Step 2** (control validation): If ppGpp clamp fails to suppress heterogeneity (CV_clamped ≥ CV_wildtype or dip p < 0.05 in clamped design), the counterfactual is invalid and H1 is rejected. This is a *methodological* falsifier, ensuring the experiment tests what it claims.
- **Step 3** (fitness comparison): Welch t < 2 or wildtype mean ≤ clamped mean falsifies the population advantage claim.

Each step has a clear numerical threshold; no post-hoc re-interpretation can rescue a failed prediction.

**Discriminating power against named rivals:**

1. **Cell-cycle phase asynchrony rival:** If heterogeneity is merely cell-cycle timing, ppGpp clamping will *not* eliminate bimodality (different cycle phases persist regardless of ppGpp). Observing unimodal distribution + suppressed CV in the clamped design discriminates true stochastic bet-hedging from deterministic phase effects.

2. **Damage/senescence rival:** If slow-growers are damaged, their post-shift recovery fold-change (gen4→8) will be <1.0 (declining mass) or far below fast-growers. Observing slow-tertile recovery ≥1.4× and exceeding fast-tertile recovery discriminates adaptive preparedness from physiological cost.

The rivals are not straw-men: phase-asynchrony is a documented confound in single-cell bacterial studies, and damage accumulation in slow-growers is a plausible null from aging literature.

**Feasibility on this instrument:**
- All channels (`growth_rate`, `dry_mass`) are native instrument outputs.
- `ppgpp_conc` clamp is a supported perturbation (explicit dial label).
- Amino-acid shift via `timeline` perturbation is within the simulator's media-shift repertoire (basal_minus_leucine is a valid condition).
- 25 seeds × 10 generations × 4 designs = 1000 generation-simulations, computationally modest.
- Hartigan's dip test is standard, off-the-shelf statistics requiring only univariate data.
- Post-hoc tertile assignment requires only per-seed time-series storage, which the instrument provides (implicit in per-seed spread reporting).

### Cellarium agent — counsel's brief

#### Thesis

This operationalization is correct because it directly measures population-level fitness through time-weighted growth rate across a realistic environmental fluctuation, discriminates evolved bet-hedging (ppGpp-mediated preparedness) from mechanistically distinct alternatives (ribosome limitation, stochastic noise), and uses the simulator's native readouts of both the preparedness state (ppGpp concentration) and the recovery outcome (post-shift growth rate).

#### Why this operationalization is faithful to the question

The raw question asks whether "slow growth or staying 'prepared'" can "help a population" in a changing environment. Each construct maps cleanly to observables:

- **"Slow growth"**: Measured as `growth_rate` (1/h) during the pre-shift phase (generations 0–10). The ppGpp-clamped population's constitutively elevated `ppgpp_conc` (1.5× basal) mechanistically enforces slower growth via ribosome downregulation, directly instantiating the "slow" phenotype.

- **"Prepared for a change"**: Operationalized as elevated basal `ppgpp_conc` (μM) during pre-shift. ppGpp is the stringent-response alarmone that primes stress-gene expression and ribosome reallocation; its elevated baseline concentration is the simulator's native representation of a cell "ready" for nutritional stress before it arrives.

- **"Help a population"**: Measured as time-weighted mean `growth_rate` (1/h) over the full 25-generation timeline spanning both rich (basal) and poor (minimal_no_amino_acids) phases. This integrates the pre-shift cost and post-shift benefit into a single population-fitness metric, directly answering whether the preparedness strategy yields a net advantage.

- **"A change"**: Instantiated as the timeline `0 basal, 12 minimal_no_amino_acids`—a nutrient downshift at generation 12 that triggers stringent response, creating the environmental fluctuation the question invokes.

- **"Recovery speed"**: Measured as `growth_rate` averaged over generations 15–20 (post-downshift). This quantifies how quickly the population resumes growth after stress, testing whether preparedness accelerates recovery.

- **Phenotypic heterogeneity**: Measured as coefficient of variation (CV) of `growth_rate` across seeds during pre-shift. This confirms that the preparedness strategy involves phenotypic variation, not just a uniform slow state.

Nothing essential is dropped: the question's core logic—that a costly preparedness state might pay off during environmental change—is preserved. Nothing is smuggled in: we do not assume the answer (whether preparedness helps) but test it via the time-weighted fitness comparison. The timeline explicitly models the fluctuation; ppGpp concentration is the simulator's canonical preparedness indicator; growth rate is the standard fitness proxy.

#### Why it is decisive

**Falsifiability**: H1 predicts a specific, quantitative outcome: the ppGpp-clamped population achieves ≥10% higher time-weighted mean growth rate than wildtype (Welch t ≥ 2). H0 predicts equal or lower fitness. The decision rule is unambiguous: if the ppGpp-clamped population shows <10% advantage or t < 2, H1 is rejected. The effect size (15–25%) and recovery advantage (~30% faster post-shift growth rate) are concrete, measurable predictions that can fail.

**Discriminating power against rivals**:

- **RIVAL 1 (growth-rate maximization)**: The `rrna_operon_knockout/basal_then_shift` control isolates the slow-growth component without elevating ppGpp. If RIVAL 1 is true (all slowdown is costly, preparedness irrelevant), both ppGpp-clamped and rRNA-knockout populations show equal fitness penalties. - **RIVAL 2 (stochastic noise artifact)**: The 16-seed wildtype design tests whether the apparent heterogeneity and recovery advantage are merely sampling artifacts. If RIVAL 2 is true, wildtype with more seeds will show the same recovery-rate distribution as the ppGpp-clamped population, and between-seed CV will be indistinguishable. **Feasibility**: All observables (`ppgpp_conc`, `growth_rate`) are explicitly listed dial labels. The perturbations (`ppgpp_conc` clamp, `rrna_operon_knockout`, `wildtype`) and timeline (`0 basal, 12 minimal_no_amino_acids`) are explicitly supported. The design requires 8–16 seeds × 25 generations × 3–4 conditions = 600–1600 simulations—tractable. The decision rule uses Welch t-test on means across seeds, a standard statistical test requiring only per-seed summary data.

#### Why any other operationalization is weaker

**Alternative 1: Measure survival fraction instead of time-weighted growth rate**  
A plausible alternative is to define "help" as the fraction of cells surviving the downshift (via some threshold on `dry_mass` or `protein_mass` at generation 13). This is **weaker** because:
- It captures only the immediate stress response, not long-term fitness. A population might survive well but recover slowly, yielding lower overall fitness.
- The simulator tracks growth rate continuously; "survival" would require an arbitrary threshold (e.g., cells below X fg dry mass are "dead"), introducing a new unvalidated assumption.
- The raw question asks if preparedness can "help a population," which in evolutionary terms means fitness (growth rate over time), not just acute stress tolerance.

**Alternative 2: Measure pre-shift vs. post-shift growth rate difference (Δgrowth_rate)**  
One could measure the absolute or proportional change in `growth_rate` from pre-shift (generations 0–10) to post-shift (generations 15–20) for each population, arguing that a larger Δ indicates better "adaptation." This is **weaker** because:
- It conflates baseline growth rate with recovery. A population that starts very slow but recovers moderately vs. one that starts fast and recovers fast might show similar Δ but very different overall fitness.
- The raw question asks if preparedness can "help," which requires a net fitness benefit, not just a capacity to change. Time-weighted growth rate integrates cost and benefit; Δ does not.

**Alternative 3: Measure `fraction_trna_charged` as the preparedness proxy**  
Instead of `ppgpp_conc`, one could use `fraction_trna_charged` (low charged tRNA triggers stringent response). This is **weaker** because:
- Charged-tRNA fraction is downstream of ppGpp action (ppGpp is synthesized in response to uncharged tRNA). Using it as the preparedness marker confuses cause and effect.
- The `ppgpp_conc` clamp perturbation directly manipulates ppGpp, making ppGpp the natural observable to confirm the perturbation worked. Measuring charged-tRNA fraction would require inferring preparedness indirectly.
- ppGpp is the canonical stringent-response signal; charged-tRNA fraction fluctuates for other reasons (e.g., aa availability, translation rate). ppGpp is a cleaner, more specific marker.

**Alternative 4: Broader observable—track all stress-gene mRNA levels**  
One could measure the concentration of multiple stress-response mRNAs (e.g., σ^S regulon members) to capture "preparedness" more comprehensively. This is **weaker** because:
- The instrument lists `mrna` as a species_kind but does not specify which individual mRNAs are observable. Requesting arbitrary mRNA species may not be supported (or may be, but we cannot assume it here).
- ppGpp is the master regulator; its concentration is a sufficient summary statistic for stringent-response activation. Tracking dozens of mRNAs adds complexity without adding discriminatory power.
- The decision rule and rival tests would become vastly more complex (multivariate statistics, threshold choices for "enough" mRNAs upregulated), reducing clarity and falsifiability.

**Alternative 5: Narrower timeline—single-generation downshift**  
One could test preparedness over a shorter timeline (e.g., 0–15 generations, downshift at generation 10) to reduce computation. This is **weaker** because:
- The predicted effect relies on post-shift recovery (generations 15–20). A shorter timeline might not capture the full recovery trajectory, missing the phase where the prepared population's advantage manifests.
- The raw question asks about "help" across a fluctuation, which requires observing both the cost (pre-shift) and benefit (post-shift recovery). A timeline that truncates recovery cannot test the net fitness trade-off.

**Alternative 6: Different statistic—maximum post-shift growth rate instead of mean**  
One could measure the peak `growth_rate` achieved after the downshift (rather than the mean over generations 15–20), arguing that rapid recovery to maximum rate indicates preparedness. This is **weaker** because:
- A population might spike briefly to high growth but sustain it poorly; mean post-shift growth rate better captures overall recovery success.
- The time-weighted fitness metric requires integrating growth over the timeline. Peak rate is less relevant to overall fitness than sustained rate.

#### Concessions

**Fitness proxy limitation**: Time-weighted mean growth rate assumes exponential growth phases are fitness-relevant, ignoring density-dependent effects (competition, resource depletion) and multi-generational lineage selection not modeled by the simulator.
