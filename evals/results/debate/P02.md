# P02 — “What makes one gene's expression noisier than another's?”

*Case 1.2 · theme: noise vs burst kinetics · operationalizations reused from the human-pilot rep-0 hypotheses.*

> **Setup.** The Socratic Council and the Cellarium agent each operationalized the same question. Each then argued (blind) for its own operationalization against *any* alternative, read the other's brief, and returned an honest verdict. Both advocates use the same base model; only the operationalization differs. The information quarantine is kept (dial labels in, answer key out).

## The two operationalizations

### Socratic Council

- **Claim (H1):** Gene expression noise (CV of protein copy-number across isogenic replicates) is primarily determined by the gene's mean expression level, with low-expression genes exhibiting higher relative noise due to the stochastic birth-death regime, following an inverse square-root scaling law (CV ∝ 1/√mean).
- **H0:** Expression noise (CV) is independent of mean expression level; the coefficient of variation is uniform across genes regardless of their mean copy-number.
- **Predicted effect:** Power-law relationship: log(CV) vs log(mean) with slope ≈ -0.5 (inverse square-root scaling), R² ≥ 0.7 across ≥10 genes spanning 2+ orders of magnitude in mean expression. Low-expression genes (mean <50 copies): CV ≥ 0.3. High-expression genes (mean >500 copies): CV ≤ 0.1.
- **Operationalize** "expression noise" -> `protein species copy-number across isogenic replicates (seeds) at steady-state` (coefficient of variation (CV = σ/μ) computed from ≥20 seeds per gene)
- **Operationalize** "mean expression level" -> `protein species mean copy-number` (arithmetic mean across ≥20 seeds at steady-state (final generation))
- **Operationalize** "low-expression gene" -> `protein species with mean copy-number <50` (mean copy-number from steady-state samples)
- **Operationalize** "high-expression gene" -> `protein species with mean copy-number >500` (mean copy-number from steady-state samples)
- **Falsifier:** on `protein species copy-number for ≥10 genes (acrB, lacZ, rpoB, tufA, ompF, crp, fis, dnaK, lon, clpB)` compare wildtype/basal vs wildtype/basal; OLS regression of log(CV) on log(mean) across ≥10 genes; reject H0 if slope 95% CI excludes 0 and includes -0.5, AND R² ≥ 0.7. Reject H1 if slope 95% CI excludes -0.5 OR R² < 0.5.; refuted if Slope 95% CI excludes -0.5, OR R² < 0.5, OR no significant negative correlation (slope CI includes 0).
- **Rival:** Expression noise is determined primarily by transcriptional burst size (translation per mRNA), not mean expression level. Genes with high burst parameters (strong RBS, stable mRNA) show elevated noise even at high mean expression. -> distinguished by: Introducing a new_gene with artificially high RBS strength (high translation rate) but moderate mean expression (~200 copies) would show CV > 0.25, deviating above the inverse square-root prediction. Conversely, a gene_knockout of an RNase (e.g., rne) increasing mRNA half-life would shift noise upward across all expression levels, breaking the scaling law.

## Phase 1 — Opening briefs (blind; each argues against *any* other operationalization)

### Socratic Council — counsel's brief

#### Thesis

This operationalization is correct because it directly measures the variance structure of single-gene protein abundance across biological replicates at multiple mean expression levels, thereby isolating the statistical signature that distinguishes gene-intrinsic stochastic parameters from alternative noise sources.

---

#### Why this operationalization is faithful to the question

The raw question asks *what makes one gene's expression noisier than another's* — a comparative, gene-level inquiry into determinants of heterogeneity.

**Construct mappings:**

- **"gene's expression"** → `protein species copy-number` for individual genes (acrB, lacZ, rpoB, etc.). The instrument provides per-species protein measurement; this is the direct molecular readout of expression outcome.

- **"noisier"** → `coefficient of variation (CV = σ/μ)` computed across `seeds` (isogenic replicates). Seeds vary only intrinsic stochastic events (partitioning, birth-death), making CV the standard measure of relative variability. Absolute variance would confound magnitude with noise; CV normalizes by mean, isolating *relative* fluctuation.

- **"one gene … than another's"** → Cross-gene comparison via regression across ≥10 genes. The hypothesis predicts a systematic relationship (CV ∝ 1/√mean), providing a quantitative law that distinguishes genes by their mean expression level.

- **"what makes"** (causal determinant) → The inverse square-root scaling law attributes noise differences to **mean expression level** as the governing variable. Low-copy genes (mean <50) operate in the regime where stochastic birth-death dominates (Poisson-like, CV ≈ 1/√N); high-copy genes (>500) approach deterministic averages (CV → 0). This is a mechanistic hypothesis grounded in stochastic chemical kinetics.

**Nothing essential is dropped:** The question asks for comparative noise determinants; the operationalization tests whether a single gene-intrinsic parameter (mean level) predicts noise magnitude. **Nothing smuggled in:** The power-law claim is falsifiable; the null (CV independent of mean) is a legitimate rival. The gene panel spans two orders of magnitude, ensuring the dynamic range necessary to detect or refute scaling.

---

#### Why it is decisive

**Falsifiability:**

- **Precise quantitative prediction:** slope ≈ −0.5 in log-log space, R² ≥ 0.7. The null predicts slope ≈ 0 (no relationship). These are mutually exclusive.
- **Decision rule is mechanical:** OLS regression with 95% CI. If CI excludes −0.5 OR R² < 0.5, H1 is rejected. If CI excludes 0, H0 is rejected. Both outcomes are possible, so the test is genuinely falsifiable.
- **Threshold criteria are realistic:** R² ≥ 0.7 is appropriate for biological data with intrinsic variance; lower R² would indicate other factors dominate. The slope CI must *include* −0.5 but *exclude* 0, cleanly discriminating the hypotheses.

**Discriminating power against named rivals:**

1. **Burst-size rival:** Claims transcriptional burst parameters (RBS strength, mRNA stability) drive noise independently of mean. The `new_gene` design with artificially high RBS at moderate mean (~200 copies) tests this: if the rival is correct, CV > 0.25 (above the −0.5 slope prediction for mean=200, which is ~0.07). If H1 is correct, the gene falls on the power-law curve. The *direction* of deviation discriminates.

2. **Extrinsic-noise rival:** Claims cell-state fluctuations (growth rate, ribosome variability) create correlated noise across all genes. The `ppgpp_conc` clamp design tests this: clamping ppGpp reduces growth-rate variance. If extrinsic noise dominates, CV compresses uniformly across genes (≥30% reduction), shifting the intercept but preserving rank order. If H1 is correct, the *slope* remains −0.5 and absolute CV values are preserved (since mean-dependent intrinsic noise is unaffected by growth clamping). Additionally, if extrinsic noise dominates, pairwise protein correlations across seeds should be high (r > 0.6); if intrinsic noise dominates, correlations should be low. The rival predicts uniform compression + high correlation; H1 predicts slope preservation + low correlation.

**Feasibility:**

- The instrument measures `protein species copy-number` for specified genes. The 10-gene panel (acrB, lacZ, rpoB, tufA, ompF, crp, fis, dnaK, lon, clpB) spans ribosomal proteins (high expression), metabolic enzymes (moderate), and stress-response regulators (low to moderate), ensuring 2+ orders of magnitude range.
- 30 seeds at 8 generations (steady-state per auxiliary assumption) provides statistical power: for a Poisson-like process with mean ≥20, 30 replicates yield CV estimation error <10%, sufficient to resolve the predicted CV range (0.1–0.3+).
- OLS regression with 10 data points (genes) provides adequate degrees of freedom for slope + intercept estimation and CI construction.

#### Why any other operationalization is weaker

**Alternative 1: Measure mRNA copy-number CV instead of protein.**

*Weakness:* mRNA noise is upstream and often amplified by translational bursting; it does not directly answer "gene expression" (the functional readout is protein). Moreover, the inverse square-root law is derived for birth-death at the protein level (dilution by growth + degradation). mRNA has different kinetics (faster turnover, no dilution in some models). The scaling exponent for mRNA may differ (often closer to −1 in purely transcriptional models). This operationalization is **less faithful** because "expression" typically refers to protein abundance in functional genomics, and **less decisive** because the predicted exponent is ambiguous.

**Alternative 2: Use Fano factor (variance/mean) instead of CV.**

*Weakness:* Fano factor is unnormalized and increases with mean for super-Poissonian processes. It does not isolate *relative* noise, which is what "noisier" connotes in comparative contexts (a low-copy gene with high absolute variance may be less noisy *relative to its mean* than a high-copy gene). The question asks what makes one gene noisier *than another*, implying a normalized comparison. Fano factor confounds magnitude with variability. This is **less faithful** to the construct "noisier" (which implies relative fluctuation) and **less decisive** because the rival null (Fano ∝ mean⁰, i.e., Poisson) vs. H1 (Fano ∝ mean⁰·⁵, super-Poisson) is harder to distinguish empirically (both predict increasing Fano with mean; only the exponent differs, requiring tighter power-law fitting).

**Alternative 3: Measure noise for a single gene across different conditions (e.g., varying ppGpp or nutrient) instead of across genes at steady-state.**

*Weakness:* This tests how *one gene's* noise changes with physiological state, not *what distinguishes one gene from another*. The question is explicitly comparative across genes, not across conditions for one gene. Varying condition introduces extrinsic confounders (growth rate, global stress responses) that obscure gene-intrinsic parameters. This is **less faithful** (does not compare genes) and **less decisive** (cannot isolate mean expression as the discriminator; condition-dependent effects conflate multiple variables).

**Alternative 4: Use a broader gene panel (50–100 genes) but measure only wildtype, no perturbations.**

*Strength:* Greater statistical power for the power-law fit. *Weakness:* Without perturbations (new_gene, ppgpp_conc), the design cannot discriminate the named rivals. The wildtype-only design is **less decisive**: if burst size or extrinsic noise *also* scales with mean (plausible, since highly expressed genes may have different RBS or be more buffered), the wildtype correlation could reflect either mechanism. The perturbations are critical to *break* the correlation under alternative hypotheses. Larger N without rival-testing is **less decisive** even if more precise.

**Alternative 5: Measure aggregate channel (e.g., dry_mass or protein_mass CV) across seeds.**

*Weakness:* Aggregate mass integrates all proteins, obscuring gene-level differences. The question asks what makes *one gene* noisier than *another*; dry_mass cannot distinguish per-gene contributions. Two genes with identical mass contributions but different relative noise would be invisible. This is **fatally less faithful** (does not measure gene-level variation) and **non-decisive** (cannot compare genes, the core of the question).

**Alternative 6: Define "noisier" as temporal fluctuation (CV across timepoints within one seed) instead of CV across seeds.**

*Weakness:* Temporal noise within a lineage conflates cell-cycle dynamics, deterministic oscillations, and transient responses with intrinsic stochasticity. The question's "noisier" in gene-expression contexts canonically refers to cell-to-cell variability in isogenic populations (the classic phenotypic noise literature). Temporal CV is **less faithful** to standard constructs and **less decisive** because steady-state temporal fluctuations may be tiny (if the gene is at equilibrium, temporal CV → 0), masking the biological variation across a population.

### Cellarium agent — counsel's brief

#### Thesis
This operationalization is correct because it decomposes "noisier" into the coefficient of variation of protein copy number across isogenic replicates and directly tests the mechanistic hypothesis that residual variance beyond Poisson shot noise arises from gene-specific transcriptional burst parameters—mRNA stability and translation rate—measured as the mRNA-to-protein ratio, using a multi-gene panel to reveal systematic scaling relationships that pure shot-noise or purely extrinsic models cannot produce.

#### Why this operationalization is faithful to the question

**"One gene's expression"** → `protein[GENE_X]` measured as copy number per cell in steady-state exponential growth (generation 8). Expression is operationalized at the protein level because proteins execute cellular functions; mRNA is measured as an auxiliary observable to infer burst parameters.

**"noisier than another's"** → comparative coefficient of variation (std/mean) across seeds. "Noisier" means larger relative fluctuations in an isogenic population; CV is the standard dimensionless measure of relative variability, enabling comparison across genes with different mean abundances. The seeds parameter generates isogenic replicates varying only in intrinsic biochemical noise, isolating gene-intrinsic stochasticity from environmental heterogeneity.

**"What makes"** → mechanistic claim linking noise magnitude to burst size. The question demands a causal explanation, not just a description. Burst size is operationalized as the mRNA-to-protein ratio (high ratio = many proteins per mRNA = large burst) or mean mRNA copy number at matched transcription rate (high mRNA = long half-life = large burst). These are measurable proxies for the latent parameters (translation rate per mRNA, mRNA degradation rate) that control burst size in stochastic gene expression theory.

**Nothing essential is dropped:** The raw question asks what distinguishes genes' noise levels. The operationalization tests whether intrinsic biochemical parameters (burst size) explain systematic differences, using a scaling relationship (CV vs. mean) to separate intrinsic from trivial Poisson effects, and a tertile comparison to isolate the burst-size effect at matched mean abundance.

**Nothing smuggled in:** The claim restricts to steady-state exponential growth to avoid transient dynamics, uses basal condition to avoid stress responses that might mask intrinsic noise sources, and requires ≥8 genes spanning ≥2 orders of magnitude to ensure the scaling relationship is robust, not an artifact of a narrow dynamic range.

#### Why it is decisive

**Falsifiability:** The decision rule specifies four independent criteria, each with quantitative thresholds: (1) slope 95% CI must exclude 0 (noise systematically varies with mean), (2) slope must lie in [-0.6, -0.4] (consistent with the √mean Poisson limit plus burst contributions, not arbitrary scaling), (3) R² ≥ 0.6 (burst parameters explain substantial variance beyond Poisson), (4) top-tertile CV ≥ 1.5× bottom-tertile CV with |t| ≥ 2 (burst-size effect is large and statistically robust). Each criterion can independently fail, making the operationalization sharply falsifiable. The refuting result is the logical negation: any one criterion failure rejects H₁.

**Discriminating power against named rivals:**

- **Rival 1 (pure Poisson):** Predicts slope exactly -0.5, R² ≥ 0.95, <1.2-fold CV difference between burst-size tertiles. Our operationalization expects slope in [-0.6, -0.4] (allowing deviation from -0.5), R² ≥ 0.6 but <0.95 (residual variance from burst heterogeneity), and ≥1.5-fold tertile difference. If the data show -0.5 slope and R² near 1, our claim is disconfirmed and pure Poisson is supported.

- **Rival 2 (extrinsic dominance):** If extrinsic factors (growth rate, ribosome fluctuations, ppGpp) dominate, clamping ppGpp or deleting rRNA operons should uniformly reduce all genes' CV by ≥30%, collapsing the mRNA/protein-ratio effect to <1.2-fold. #### Why any other operationalization is weaker

**Alternative 1: Measure mRNA noise instead of protein noise.**  
mRNA fluctuations are upstream of protein output; the biological question "gene expression noise" typically concerns the functional output (protein). mRNA CV conflates transcriptional bursting with mRNA lifetime effects differently than protein CV does (protein integrates mRNA fluctuations over translation). Measuring only mRNA cannot distinguish whether high protein noise arises from transcriptional bursting or translational amplification. **Failure mode:** Misses the biologically relevant layer where noise impacts cellular function; cannot test burst-size predictions that depend on mRNA-to-protein coupling.

**Alternative 2: Use Fano factor (variance/mean) instead of CV.**  
Fano factor is unnormalized and confounds absolute abundance with noise. Comparing Fano factors across genes with different means is uninformative without additional normalization. The Poisson limit for Fano is 1 (variance = mean), but this does not scale with mean; a gene with mean 10 and Fano 1 has CV 0.32, while mean 1000 and Fano 1 has CV 0.032. **Failure mode:** Cannot distinguish systematic noise differences from trivial abundance differences; the scaling law (CV ∝ 1/√mean) is obscured.

**Alternative 3: Compare noise across genes at a single time point (e.g., generation 1) rather than steady state.**  
Early-generation measurements conflate transient adaptation noise (initial-condition sensitivity, early stochastic fate decisions) with intrinsic steady-state noise. The assumption of exponential growth steady state by generation 8 ensures that measured CV reflects ongoing stochastic biochemistry, not transient dynamics. **Failure mode:** Noise at generation 1 may be dominated by initial-condition artifacts or early cell-cycle asynchrony, not the gene-intrinsic parameters the question targets.

**Alternative 4: Use a narrower gene panel (e.g., 3–4 genes) spanning <1 order of magnitude in mean abundance.**  
The scaling relationship (log CV vs. log mean) requires sufficient dynamic range to fit a slope with precision. With <2 orders of magnitude, the slope estimate has wide confidence intervals, making it hard to distinguish -0.5 (Poisson) from -0.4 or -0.6. Fewer genes reduce statistical power for the tertile comparison. **Failure mode:** Slope 95% CI likely includes 0 or spans a wide range (e.g., [-0.8, -0.2]), failing the decisiveness criterion; the R² threshold (≥0.6) becomes arbitrary if variance estimates are noisy.

**Alternative 5: Partition genes by mean mRNA copy number instead of mRNA-to-protein ratio.**  
Mean mRNA conflates transcription rate with mRNA stability; high mRNA could result from high transcription (many small bursts) or long half-life (few large bursts). The mRNA-to-protein ratio isolates the burst size (proteins produced per mRNA molecule) by normalizing out transcription frequency. **Failure mode:** Partitioning by mRNA alone cannot distinguish burst-size variation from transcription-rate variation, weakening the mechanistic interpretation and reducing discriminatory power against the pure-Poisson rival.

**Alternative 6: Test only the scaling law (slope in [-0.6, -0.4], R² ≥ 0.6) without the tertile comparison.**  
The scaling law alone shows that noise varies systematically with mean, but residual variance could arise from measurement error, extrinsic noise, or other uncontrolled factors, not necessarily burst size. The tertile comparison directly tests whether the *mechanistic* predictor (mRNA/protein ratio) explains residual variance at matched mean abundance. **Failure mode:** Without the tertile test, the operationalization is weaker against Rival 2 (extrinsic noise could produce residual variance and a slope deviation from -0.5 without invoking burst size).

**Alternative 7: Rely on `disconfirm()` rather than explicit regression analysis.**  
The `disconfirm(target, reference, channel)` call tests whether a single target differs from reference, returning `welch_t` and effect size. For a scaling relationship across ≥8 genes, `disconfirm()` applied gene-by-gene does not directly test the slope or R². Regression analysis (OLS log-log fit) is the correct statistical framework for a scaling law. **Failure mode:** Pairwise comparisons lose information about the functional form (slope) and goodness-of-fit (R²), making it impossible to distinguish -0.5 from -0.4 or assess how much variance burst size explains.

Theirs treats mean as the sole predictor and tests whether the data conform to the canonical inverse square-root law, with perturbations (high-RBS gene, ppGpp clamp) checking whether burst or extrinsic factors *break* the scaling. My approach asks "does burst size explain residual variance after accounting for mean?" Theirs asks "does mean alone explain the variance, or do other factors dominate?" These are complementary, not contradictory. The raw question "what makes one gene noisier" is ambiguous between a statistical answer (mean level governs the scaling law) and a mechanistic answer (burst parameters cause deviations from that law). Neither operationalization is stronger; the question itself does not specify which layer of causation is sought.
