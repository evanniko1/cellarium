### Socratic Council — counsel's brief

#### Thesis

This operationalization is correct because it directly measures the observable hallmark of preferential substrate utilization—biphasic exponential growth with an intervening lag—using time-resolved growth rate dynamics that discriminate sequential consumption from simultaneous co-utilization or toxicity-driven artifacts.

#### Why this operationalization is faithful to the question

The raw question "How do bacteria choose between two sugars in the same flask?" contains three essential constructs:

1. **"Choose"** → operationalized as **preferential sequential utilization** rather than simultaneous consumption. Mapped to the presence of two distinct exponential growth phases (two local maxima in `growth_rate`) separated by a trough, detected via `scipy.signal.find_peaks`. This directly tests whether the cell prioritizes one substrate over the other in time.

2. **"Between two sugars"** → operationalized as **glucose + acetate mixture** (`timeline: '0 minimal_GLC_10mM_AC_5mM'`). Both substrates are initialized simultaneously at t=0, creating the ecological context of choice. The glucose-only control (`timeline: '0 minimal_GLC_10mM'`) isolates whether the biphasic pattern is intrinsic to glucose metabolism or requires the presence of acetate.

3. **"In the same flask"** → operationalized as **simultaneous availability from t=0**, not sequential addition. The timeline perturbation initializes both carbon sources together, mirroring the experimental setup where bacteria encounter both substrates at once.

The key claim—**diauxic growth**—is the canonical bacterial "choice" behavior: catabolite repression causes the cell to consume the preferred substrate (glucose) first, then switch to the secondary substrate (acetate) after depletion. This is operationalized through **five observables**:

- **Biphasic growth pattern**: `growth_rate` time-series exhibits two local maxima (≥0.2 /h prominence, ≥2 generation separation) with a trough dropping ≥50% from the first peak, then recovering to ≥70% of trough value. This is the signature of sequential substrate use.
- **Fast glucose phase**: Mean `growth_rate` during generations 0–3 ≥0.8 /h, consistent with glucose-supported exponential growth.
- **Slow acetate phase**: Mean `growth_rate` during generations 6–10 is 0.5–0.6 /h, ~30–40% lower than glucose phase, consistent with acetate's slower catabolism.
- **Lag phase**: Duration where `growth_rate` remains <50% of first peak, occurring between generations 3–7, spanning ~1–2 generations. This reflects metabolic reprogramming (induction of acetate-utilization operons).
- **Biomass trajectory**: `dry_mass` shows sigmoidal biphasic accumulation with reduced slope during lag, providing independent confirmation of the growth transition.

Nothing essential is dropped: the question asks "how" bacteria choose, and diauxie is the mechanistic answer (catabolite repression → sequential use). Nothing is smuggled in: we do not assume *which* sugar is preferred (that is an empirical prediction), nor do we claim to measure gene expression directly (outside instrument scope). The operationalization is agnostic to mechanism (CRP–cAMP, etc.) and relies solely on growth phenotype.

#### Why it is decisive

**Falsifiability**: H1 makes six quantitative predictions that can individually or jointly falsify it:
1. Two local maxima in `growth_rate` (not one, not zero).
2. Trough ≥50% below first peak.
3. Recovery to ≥70% of trough value.
4. Welch t-test |t| ≥2 between early (gen 0–3) and late (gen 6–10) phases.
5. Effect size ≥30% reduction in growth rate.
6. Lag duration 1–2 generations.

If any of these fail, H1 is rejected. The decision rule is algorithmic (`scipy.signal.find_peaks` with fixed parameters) and leaves no subjective wiggle room. H0 specifies the null pattern: monotonic exponential growth (single peak, or monotonic decline without recovery, or <20% variation). This is mutually exclusive with H1.

**Discriminating power against rivals**:

- **Rival 1 (simultaneous co-utilization)**: Predicts single exponential phase, no lag, `growth_rate` CV <20% across generations 0–10. The biphasic peak-finding algorithm directly refutes this: if two peaks are detected, co-utilization is ruled out. The Welch t-test provides statistical rigor (|t| ≥2 ensures the two phases differ beyond stochastic noise).

- **Rival 2 (acetate toxicity/overflow metabolism)**: Predicts the lag arises from acetate secreted *during* glucose growth, not from regulated substrate choice. The **glucose-only control** (`timeline: '0 minimal_GLC_10mM'`) is the critical discriminator: if glucose alone produces a biphasic pattern (overflow acetate → re-import lag), the control will show two peaks. If the control shows monotonic growth (CV <20%, ≤1 peak), the lag in the mixture is attributable to the *switch* between exogenous substrates, not overflow toxicity. This design isolates catabolite repression from overflow metabolism.

**Feasibility**: The instrument provides `growth_rate` at fine temporal resolution ("instantaneous specific growth rate"), enabling detection of transient lag phases. Ten generations (~5–10 hours for E. coli) are sufficient to observe both exponential phases and the intervening lag. Five seeds ensure statistical power to detect a 30% effect size (Welch t ≥2) while averaging over intrinsic noise. The `dry_mass` channel provides an independent biomass readout to corroborate the growth rate pattern.

- **Decisive point:** Opposing counsel's use of extracellular substrate concentrations (GLC[e], AC[e]) as direct observables to verify the timing and mechanism of substrate depletion, combined with CRP protein as a mechanistic anchor, provides stronger evidentiary grounding than my purely growth-rate-based inference of substrate dynamics.

- **Rationale:** Both operationalizations correctly identify diauxic growth as the answer to "how bacteria choose between sugars" and use similar biphasic growth-rate signatures as the primary observable. However, opposing counsel's design is methodologically superior in three concrete ways. First, they directly measure extracellular metabolite concentrations (GLC[e], AC[e]) to verify substrate availability and depletion timing, whereas I infer substrate dynamics solely from growth-rate phenotype—a weaker evidential chain given that my own brief acknowledges "available channels do not include exchange fluxes or extracellular metabolite concentrations." Second, they measure CRP protein concentration as a mechanistic checkpoint linking catabolite repression to the observed lag, strengthening the causal interpretation; my operationalization remains "mechanism-agnostic," which I defended as a virtue but which actually sacrifices discriminating power when distinguishing catabolite repression from other lag-producing mechanisms. Third, their falsifier is more robust: requiring 80% of seeds (≥8/10) to individually meet the biphasic criteria guards against stochastic false positives better than my pooled-analysis approach with 5 seeds. My critique of their substrate-concentration approach in my own brief ("concentration trajectories lack a natural null") is undermined by their actual design, which uses basal monotonic growth (CV <0.2) as the reference—the same logic I employed. Their operationalization is not weaker for measuring substrates; it is stronger for measuring substrates *in addition to* growth rate, providing convergent evidence.

### Cellarium agent — **VERDICT: MAINTAIN**

- **Decisive point:** Opposing counsel's decision rule applies scipy.signal.find_peaks to the growth_rate time series, but the min_distance=2 generations parameter makes it blind to lag durations shorter than ~2 generations (60-120 minutes), whereas diauxic lags can be as brief as 0.5-1.0 generation (~15-30 minutes in fast growth). This arbitrary threshold risks false negatives—failing to detect legitimate biphasic patterns with compact lags—and is not calibrated against any basal reference that would establish the CV/noise floor for distinguishing transient troughs from stochastic fluctuations.

- **Rationale:** Opposing counsel's operationalization is strong: the biphasic peak-detection algorithm is explicit, the glucose-only control (design 3) elegantly isolates Rival 2 (overflow toxicity), and the Welch t-test between early/late phases quantifies the magnitude of the shift. However, the decision rule's min_distance=2 generations is a single-point-of-failure: if E. coli's actual diauxic lag in the simulator is ~0.8 generations (plausible for a well-adapted strain), the peak-finder will merge the two exponential phases into a single broad peak and reject H1 even when diauxie occurs. My operationalization avoids this by computing the coefficient of variation (CV) and trough/peak ratio (R) over a sliding window, requiring CV ≥0.3 and R ≤0.5 across ≥80% of seeds—criteria that adapt to lag duration and are validated against the basal reference (CV <0.2). Opposing counsel also uses 5 seeds vs. my 10, reducing power to detect the ≥80% threshold if seed-to-seed variability is high. Their glucose-only control is superior to my approach (which lacks a direct overflow test), but the core falsifier is more brittle.

## Outcome

**Cellarium agent prevails.** The Socratic Council conceded that the Cellarium agent's operationalization is stronger; the agent did not concede.

*Impartial synthesis.* The two counsel reached opposing verdicts, indicating the debate did not produce a clean consensus on which operationalization is stronger. The Socratic Council conceded (YIELD) on the grounds that the opposing design added direct measurement of extracellular substrate concentrations and CRP protein as a mechanistic anchor, plus a more robust per-seed falsifier, thereby providing convergent evidence beyond a purely growth-rate-based inference. The Cellarium agent maintained its position (MAINTAIN) by identifying a distinct vulnerability: the opposing design's peak-detection rule relies on a fixed min_distance=2 generations parameter that could miss short diauxic lags, and it uses fewer seeds, making its core falsifier more brittle than a CV/trough-ratio criterion calibrated to a basal reference. Because each side's decisive point targets a different dimension—evidentiary breadth and mechanistic anchoring on one hand versus decision-rule robustness and statistical power on the other—the exchange highlights genuine trade-offs rather than establishing one operationalization as unambiguously superior. On balance, the question surfaced complementary strengths and weaknesses in both designs, so the verdicts jointly point to a partial rather than decisive separation.
